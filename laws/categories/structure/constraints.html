CONSTRAINT CLASSES

TAP TO EXPAND
1) Capacity Limits of throughput, latency, and load.
›

Capacity answers one question: how much can this system carry before behavior changes?

Throughput

Throughput is how much work can be completed per unit time without backlog growth.

  • Stable throughput keeps queues short.
  • Overrun throughput creates hidden shortcuts and exceptions.
  • Queue growth is evidence of capacity mismatch.
Latency

Latency is how long correction takes. Long latency hides failure until it is expensive.

  • Slow feedback increases drift.
  • Slow repair turns small errors into cascades.
  • Short feedback loops preserve coherence.
Saturation

Saturation is the point where the system begins trading correctness for speed.

  • “Just this once” becomes default.
  • Standards soften under pressure.
  • Unseen work moves off-ledger.
Capacity failure signature

When capacity is exceeded, the system becomes unpredictable.

  • Inconsistent decisions
  • Blame-shifting
  • More meetings, less output
  • Shortcuts becoming policy

Capacity is bounded service under load. The metric is not intention; it is queue stability.

Service rate vs arrival rate

When arrival rate exceeds service rate, backlog diverges. Divergence is not a narrative—it's a structural fact.

Latency as stability parameter

Correction latency increases variance. Variance increases drift. Drift increases failure probability.

Saturation threshold

Saturation is where error-correction can no longer keep pace. Past this point, failures appear “sudden.”

Containment rule

Healthy systems detect overload early and shed load locally, preventing cascade.

2) Resources Time, attention, energy, and tooling limits.
›

Resources answer: what inputs are finite and therefore must be governed.

Time

Time is a hard constraint. Compressing it increases error and forces tradeoffs.

  • Short timelines reward shortcuts.
  • Long timelines reward discipline.
  • Unrealistic timelines are hidden coercion.
Attention

Attention is the scarce resource that governs whether constraints remain real.

  • What is unobserved drifts.
  • Over-attention creates pressure and distortion.
  • Proper attention creates stable correction loops.
Energy

Energy is the capacity to sustain standards over time.

  • Fatigue is a reliability risk.
  • Burnout is structural failure.
  • Rest is a governance tool, not indulgence.
Tooling

Tooling determines whether work is inspectable and repeatable.

  • Without tooling, evidence becomes story.
  • Bad tools create invisible drift.
  • Good tools preserve receipts.

Resource constraints are bounded inputs. They define feasibility, not preference.

Budgeted inputs

Time/attention/energy/tooling are allocatable but finite. Optimization without bounds produces fragility.

Attention as measurement bandwidth

Limited attention reduces observability. Reduced observability increases hidden state and error accumulation.

Energy as sustained control

Control requires sustained effort. When effort drops below minimum, the system transitions to drift.

Tooling as audit surface

Tooling creates artifact trails. Artifact absence forces narrative substitution.

3) Incentives Reward structures that shape behavior.
›

Incentives answer: what behavior will repeat regardless of declared values.

Reward

Rewarded behavior becomes the true platform, even if it contradicts stated rules.

  • Reward outcomes you can inspect.
  • Do not reward theatre.
  • Do not reward speed at the cost of coherence.
Penalty

Punishment changes behavior by hiding it. Penalize carefully and transparently.

  • Hidden mistakes become future disasters.
  • Public repair prevents drift.
  • Fear-based penalties create fragmentation.
Visibility

What gets measured gets optimized. What isn’t measured gets ignored.

  • Metrics without invariants invite gaming.
  • Visibility must include costs and externalities.
Incentive failure signature

When incentives invert, the system looks coherent and performs incoherently.

  • Rules praised but violated
  • Results defended with stories
  • Blame loops replace repair

Incentives are objective functions. Misalignment produces local optimization and global collapse.

Objective mismatch

Local objectives diverge from global constraints → fragmentation grows.

Metric gaming

Any measurable target becomes gameable unless constrained by invariant checks.

Hidden externalities

If external costs aren’t measured, “success” can be net failure.

Stability rule

Incentives must be constrained so the easiest path remains the coherent one.

4) Discipline How constraints stay real under pressure.
›

Discipline is the difference between “we have standards” and “our standards survive pressure.”

Explicitness

Hidden rules become politics. Explicit rules become governance.

  • Say what matters.
  • Say what stops.
  • Say what counts as done.
Consistency

Rules that change by audience are not rules.

  • Same constraint for every case.
  • Same repair for every breach.
Repair speed

Fast repair prevents small fractures from becoming identity conflicts.

  • Own deviation quickly.
  • Correct without blame.
Discipline failure signature

When discipline fails, exceptions become precedent and precedent becomes the real system.

  • “Just this once” becomes routine
  • Standards become optional
  • Repair becomes performative

Discipline is constraint enforcement under load. It is the mechanism preventing drift.

Receipt-gated progression

Advancement requires evidence. Evidence prevents narrative shortcuts.

Invalidation on upstream change

If assumptions change, downstream states are revoked. This preserves coherence.

Bounded variance

Discipline reduces variance across contexts by keeping constraints stable.

Stop conditions

Discipline includes legitimate termination. Endless continuation is incoherent.
