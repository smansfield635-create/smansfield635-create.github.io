Constraints

Structure · What holds because it must.

Constraints are the rules you don’t get to negotiate with. They define what is possible, what is stable, and what will break.

1) Capacity constraints

Capacity limits define how much load a system can carry before behavior changes.

Throughput

How much the system can process per unit time before backlog or delay appears.

Bandwidth

How many simultaneous demands can be handled without collision.

Latency

How long feedback takes; long latency hides failure until it’s expensive.

Failure mode

Overload creates shortcuts, exceptions, and drift.

2) Resource constraints

Resources are not just money. They include time, attention, energy, and tooling.

Time

Deadlines are structural; compressing time increases error.

Attention

What isn’t watched drifts.

Energy

Effort is finite; sustained strain reveals true structure.

Failure mode

When resources thin, standards soften unless protected.

3) Incentive constraints

Incentives shape behavior more reliably than intent.

Reward

What is rewarded becomes repeated.

Penalty

What is punished becomes hidden.

Visibility

What is measured becomes optimized.

Failure mode

Bad incentives produce coherent-looking narratives and incoherent outcomes.

4) Constraint discipline

Structures hold when constraints are explicit and consistently enforced.

Explicitness

Hidden constraints create surprise and blame.

Consistency

Rules that change by audience are not rules.

Repair

Deviation must be corrected quickly or it becomes precedent.

Failure mode

Exceptions become the real structure.

Constraints are invariants and bounds that govern admissible state transitions.

1) Hard vs soft constraints
Hard

Physical / logical impossibilities; violation forces failure.

Soft

Optimization preferences; violation increases cost.

Guarded

Constraints enforced by gates/receipts.

Failure mode

Soft constraints treated as hard (or vice versa) creates instability.

2) Load and stability
Load

Stress applied to a system (traffic, demand, variance).

Stability

Bounded response under load.

Failure threshold

When correction can’t keep up with accumulation.

Failure mode

Latent instability appears “sudden” after long drift.

3) Constraint visibility
Measurement

Without measurement rules, constraint enforcement is arbitrary.

Audit

Constraints must be inspectable to remain invariant.

Receipt

Advancement requires evidence of constraint satisfaction.

Failure mode

Invisible constraints become narrative weapons.

4) Constraint-preserving adaptation
Adapt

Change policy while preserving invariants.

Drift

Change invariants without acknowledging it.

Invalidation

Downstream states revoked when upstream assumptions change.

Failure mode

Adaptation becomes drift when receipts are absent.
