Closed loop navigator

The loop is the technology.

Step 1 Capture

Where does the water come from? Capture collects water from available sources: surface flow, runoff, stored reserves, condensation, atmospheric recovery, or controlled intake.

Step 2 Screen

What physical load enters? Screening removes debris, sediment, grit, large particulates, and incoming material that would overload later treatment stages.

Step 3 Purify

What threats must be reduced? Purification addresses biological, chemical, mineral, and particulate risks according to the mission requirement.

Step 4 Balance

What chemistry must be restored? Balanced water may need pH, mineral, salinity, temperature, or other chemistry corrections before use or return.

Step 5 Store

How is the reserve protected? Storage guards water against loss, leakage, freezing, contamination, stagnation, pressure change, and emergency depletion.

Step 6 Use

What systems consume water? Use includes drinking, food systems, hygiene, cooling, agriculture, habitat systems, and industrial support.

Step 7 Recover

What water can be collected after use? Recovery captures greywater, wastewater, condensate, runoff, vapor, and controlled discharge.

Step 8 Reclaim

What can be separated from waste? Reclamation separates usable water from salts, organics, solids, contaminants, brine, and process residues.

Step 9 Return

Where does the water go next? Returned water moves back into storage, habitat systems, agriculture, atmospheric exchange, or planetary cycle.

System pressures

In an open system, mistakes can be diluted or flushed away. In a closed system, mistakes come back.

Engineering notes

Technical language for deeper readers.

Engineering appendix Open technical depth.
Stream classification Every stream needs a name. Clean, grey, black, brine, condensate, runoff, atmospheric, process, and reserve streams must not be mixed without purpose.
Contaminant tracking Removal is not disappearance. Contaminants must be tracked, concentrated, isolated, neutralized, reused, or safely stored.
Storage protection Reserve water can fail quietly. Storage must guard against stagnation, leakage, freezing, pressure change, contamination, and hidden depletion.
Mineral balancing Chemistry must match use. Water that is clean enough for machinery may not be balanced for habitat, agriculture, or drinking pathways.
Recovery efficiency The loop needs math. Recovered volume, quality, energy cost, rejection mass, and reset cost determine whether the loop can endure.
Sensor gates Measurement authorizes movement. Water should not advance to use, storage, habitat return, or atmospheric return without measurement gates.
Atmospheric return capacity Air has limits. Humidity, vapor, condensation, and atmospheric water cycling must not exceed capture, control, or habitat tolerance.
Loop trust criteria The system must prove itself. The loop is trusted only when source, treatment, storage, use, recovery, rejection, and return remain measurable.
Kill conditions

The system stops if the loop cannot be trusted.

Safety stop Open the failure conditions.
  • Stop if water cannot be measured.
  • Stop if water cannot be tracked.
  • Stop if contaminants cannot be separated.
  • Stop if wastewater cannot be recovered.
  • Stop if storage cannot be protected.
  • Stop if mineral balance cannot be controlled.
  • Stop if more water is lost than returned.
  • Stop if the habitat depends on an untested water stream.
  • Stop if atmospheric return exceeds capture capacity.
  • Stop if emergency reserves are undefined.
  • Stop if clear water is treated as automatically safe.
  • Stop if the narrative turns closed-loop science into magic.
