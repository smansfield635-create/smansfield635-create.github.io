Authority / Route Owner and location must exist before the claim expands.

A law category has to know who owns it, where it lives, and where it sends the reader before it can make a larger claim. Without that, a page can look official while floating without real authority.

Authority / Route requires explicit parent route, route identity, owner function, construct class, inbound path, outbound path, return path, and verification path before a public claim can be treated as governed.

Proof / Receipt Checkable evidence and records outrank confidence.

Proof keeps the system honest. A claim needs something inspectable, and a major renewal needs a receipt so the next audit can see what changed, what stayed in place, and what still has not been proven.

Proof / Receipt binds evidence hierarchy, diagnostic posture, route verification, deployment trace, changed scope, preserved scope, dependency posture, and visual-pass status into an auditable record.

Runtime / Animation Motion serves structure; it does not replace it.

Animation can guide attention and make hierarchy easier to feel, but it cannot replace meaning. A visitor should still understand the page if motion is reduced, paused, or removed.

Runtime / Animation constrains motion to authorized route classes, preserves reduced-motion behavior, prevents heavy runtime leakage into static law pages, and blocks animation from becoming proof or source truth.

Generation / Visual Claim A better-looking page is not automatically a proven page.

A page can look stronger without being finished. Visual improvement, source readiness, generation status, proof, and public completion must remain separate so beauty does not impersonate truth.

Generation / Visual Claim separates baseline state, readiness label, source truth, visual approval, runtime stability, proof state, and final completion into non-collapsing status fields.

Human Boundary / Interaction Support must not become reckless authority.

The system can explain, organize, prepare, and support, but it must not pretend to replace qualified authority where qualified authority is required. Interactive controls must also clearly show what can be touched and what cannot.

Human Boundary / Interaction preserves high-stakes claim boundaries, qualified-verification requirements, control legibility, affordance clarity, non-impersonation rules, and separation between assistance and formal authority.

Right side Scientific Law Catalog

This side points into Scientific Law and explains the branch as the reality-test system.

This side maps scientific-law objects, reality-test controls, and claim-maturity states under the Laws branch.

Theory A testable explanation that risks correction.

Theory gives the claim a possible explanation, but the explanation must remain answerable to reality. It becomes useful only when it produces expectations that can be checked and corrected.

Theory is a model framework with declared assumptions, objects, variables, expected behavior, falsifiability exposure, explanatory boundary, and revision conditions.

Evidence A checkable record with provenance.

Evidence is the part of the claim that can be inspected after confidence, preference, memory, and persuasion are removed. It does not have to prove everything, but it must honestly show what it can support.

Evidence is recorded observation with provenance, inspection value, relevance to the claim boundary, repeatability value, independence posture, and separation from interpretation.

Measure A defined coordinate system for comparison.

Measurement makes the claim less vague. It says what is being measured, how it is being measured, what unit is being used, and how much uncertainty may travel with the result.

Measurement defines the object, variable, unit, method, calibration state, sampling frame, detection limit, comparison standard, uncertainty range, and acceptable error boundary.

Limits The boundary that protects truth from overclaiming.

Limits keep a claim honest. They say what the evidence supports, what it does not support, what remains uncertain, and where the claim should stop.

Limits declare confidence range, uncertainty range, domain constraint, sampling constraint, instrument constraint, model applicability, unresolved variables, and revision conditions.

Scientific Route The reality-check path from impression to bounded claim.

A claim should not jump from first impression to final certainty. It moves through observation, definition, measurement, comparison, revision, and limitation so reality can correct it.

Scientific Route defines sequential claim maturation through observation record, boundary definition, measurement method, comparative test, model revision, and explicit limitation.

Repeatability / Falsifiability Another pass and the possibility of being wrong.

A result needs to survive another pass, and a serious theory must be able to lose. If no result could ever count against the claim, the claim is not being tested by reality.

Repeatability requires comparable results across attempts, observers, instruments, or conditions within a declared tolerance. Falsifiability requires a condition that could contradict, narrow, revise, or reject the claim.

Calibration / Uncertainty The tool must be accountable and error must remain visible.

The method or instrument must be trusted before the result is trusted. Uncertainty does not destroy knowledge; it protects knowledge from pretending to be more exact than it is.

Calibration evaluates method, instrument, observer, unit, reference standard, and comparison stability. Uncertainty declares measurement error, sampling limits, model mismatch, environmental variation, and unresolved variables.

Causality / Model Scope Cause requires controls, and every model has a range.

Cause requires more than “this happened after that.” A model can also be useful inside one range and misleading somewhere else, so the boundary has to travel with it.

Causality requires control logic, mechanism review, comparison, and alternative-explanation testing. Model Scope defines domain, inputs, assumptions, exclusions, constraints, and operating range.

Claim Testing Ladder The maturity path from impression to scientific claim.

The ladder prevents a claim from skipping the hard parts. Impression becomes observation, observation becomes evidence, evidence becomes measurement, and only then can a bounded scientific claim emerge.

Claim Testing Ladder maps maturity states from impression to observation, evidence, measurement, repeatability, model, limitation, and scientific claim with scope, uncertainty, and correction preserved.
