Consistency

Integrity · Alignment across time.

Consistency is doing the same right thing for the same reason, even when the situation changes.

It is the first visible sign of integrity because it removes “version-switching.”

Private vs public

Consistency is confirmed when your standards at home match your standards in public.

If your rules change when witnesses change, integrity becomes performance.

Under pressure

Pressure exposes what you actually believe.

Consistency is staying aligned when fear, ego, money, or urgency tries to buy an exception.

Common failure mode

“I’m different in different environments” becomes an excuse for contradiction.

Consistency replaces that with a single standard.

Consistency signature: fewer explanations, fewer exceptions, and repeatable behavior across contexts.

Consistency is output stability under comparable inputs and constraints.

It is measured by variance reduction across time windows and environments.

Repeatability

If the same conditions produce different outputs, the system is inconsistent.

Repeatability converts stories into inspectable evidence.

Drift

Inconsistency often appears as drift: definitions change, incentives change, measurement changes.

Consistency requires invariant measurement rules.

Stability under perturbation

Stable systems return quickly after disturbance.

Signature: bounded variance + short correction latency.

Consistency signature: bounded variance across contexts with stable observer classification.

CANONICAL LAWS (INTEGRITY)

  • Consistency Principle — identical conditions must produce identical outcomes.
  • Constraint Preservation — standards remain stable when incentives change.
