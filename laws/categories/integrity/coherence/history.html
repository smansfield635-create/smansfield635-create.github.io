Coherence · where it appears.

Intro

Coherence shows up wherever parts stop fighting and start behaving like one system. In people, it looks like alignment across time: what you say, do, and produce stays consistent.

Four paragraphs

Coherence appears early as small honesty loops: choosing truth when it costs, and closing gaps before they widen.

It becomes visible socially when others can predict you without controlling you. Trust forms because the rule-set doesn’t drift.

It fails historically through rationalization and exceptions: changing standards to protect identity instead of protecting alignment.

It scales through repetition: one aligned person → one aligned relationship → one aligned team → stable institutions.

Close

History matters because coherence is trackable. You can see it accumulate, and you can see it collapse.

Intro

Coherence appears when constraints dominate noise and a system exhibits consistent behavior across perturbations. It is a stability signature: outputs remain compatible with declared constraints.

Four paragraphs

Early coherence emerges as internal consistency: fewer contradictions, tighter feedback, and reduced variance under repeated inputs.

It becomes observable as reproducibility: independent observers converge on the same classification from the same evidence.

It collapses through constraint drift: changing rules, hidden substitutions, and unstable feedback amplify divergence.

It scales when invariants hold across levels: unit → coupling → network → institution, without violating core constraints.

Close

History matters because coherence leaves artifacts. If coherence is real, it remains detectable across time and conditions.

Canonical laws (Coherence)

  • Coherence Constraint Law — external coherence cannot exceed internal self-coherence.
  • Fragmentation Detection Law — misalignment between stated values, executed actions, and produced outcomes indicates loss of coherence.
