Five Flags

Five Flags is like charades, except the clue-giver cannot act it out or explain it. The Flag Master can only guide the room with flags, and the players have to read the signal before the meaning disappears.

Return to Products Open Showroom Read Gauges

Theme

Identity made visible under pressure.

Five Flags is not flag trivia, politics, civics homework, or a collecting game. It is a signal-reading game about what a flag communicates when words are limited. The pressure is simple: the Flag Master knows the card, but cannot simply tell the table what it is.

The game asks the room to notice meaning, direction, confidence, hesitation, correction, and arrival. A flag becomes more than decoration. It becomes the only language allowed.

Role 1

Referee

The Referee controls the card, shows it to the Flag Master, watches the round, and confirms when the guessers hit the target. The Referee keeps the game honest.

Role 2

Flag Master

The Flag Master receives the card from the Referee. They cannot explain verbally. They can only use flags to guide the guessers toward or away from the answer.

Role 3

Guessers

Guessers interpret the Flag Master’s signals. They call out guesses, adjust direction, read the table, and try to hit the nail on the head.

Minimum table

Three players minimum.

Five Flags needs at least three people because the core triangle matters: one person controls truth, one person carries the signal, and one person has to interpret. More guessers can join, but the minimum structure is Referee, Flag Master, and Guesser.

Round loop

Referee shows. Flag Master signals. Guessers interpret.

1 · Draw The Referee draws or selects a card. The card may contain a word, phrase, value, identity, person, place, object, prompt, or idea depending on the version being played.
2 · Show The Referee shows the card to the Flag Master only. The guessers do not see it.
3 · Signal The Flag Master uses flags to guide the table. They are not explaining; they are signaling direction.
4 · Guess Guessers call out answers, revise their thinking, and follow the Flag Master’s feedback.
5 · Correct The Flag Master keeps signaling: colder, warmer, closer, wrong direction, almost there, or hit the nail on the head.
6 · Confirm The Referee confirms the successful answer when the guessers land on the card.
Signal

Colder

The table is moving away from the target.

Signal

Warmer

The table is moving in the right direction.

Signal

Closer

The table is near the meaning but not there yet.

Signal

Wrong way

The table needs to change direction before it locks onto the wrong idea.

Signal

Nailed it

The table has hit the answer cleanly.

Example round

One card. No explanation. Only flags.

The Referee shows the Flag Master a card. The guessers start broad. The Flag Master shows a flag that tells them they are cold. A guesser changes direction. The Flag Master signals warmer. The table starts narrowing the meaning. Another guess gets close, but not exact. The Flag Master signals almost there. Someone finally says the right word or phrase. The Referee confirms the hit.

The fun is not only in guessing. The fun is in watching the room learn how to read a person who is not allowed to explain themselves.

Versions

Same mechanic. Different card pressure.

Kids, teen, and adult versions do not change the core game. The Referee still shows the card. The Flag Master still cannot explain. Guessers still read the flags. What changes is the complexity of the cards and the pressure of the interpretation.

Kids version

Simple words, pictures, and feelings.

The kids version uses concrete cards: animals, places, emotions, actions, colors, classroom situations, family moments, and simple values. The point is to make signal-reading playful and safe.

  • Best for families, classrooms, youth groups, and parties.
  • Cards stay visual, concrete, and easy to act on.
  • The table learns listening, guessing, correction, and confidence.
Teen version

Identity, pressure, and social meaning.

The teen version adds harder cards: reputation, belonging, online behavior, inside jokes, social pressure, school life, friendship tension, confidence, and identity.

  • Best for youth groups, classrooms, team-building, and social play.
  • Cards require sharper interpretation without becoming heavy-handed.
  • The room practices reading tone, confidence, and symbolic meaning.
Adult version

Abstract ideas, culture, and layered meaning.

The adult version can include abstract ideas, values, conflict, brands, culture, public figures, relationship dynamics, work situations, and layered prompts.

  • Best for parties, workshops, retreats, team-building, and conversation rooms.
  • Cards can be funny, strategic, personal, cultural, or philosophical.
  • The table has to read meaning under pressure without overexplaining.
What it brings

A new signal-reading party game.

Five Flags gives people the fun of charades without copying charades. The limitation is different: the Flag Master is not silent because they are acting; they are silent because the flag is the language.

Why it is unique

The clue is not the answer. The clue is the signal.

The game turns feedback itself into the mechanic. Players are not only guessing a card. They are learning how to read direction, confidence, correction, and arrival from a symbolic signal.
