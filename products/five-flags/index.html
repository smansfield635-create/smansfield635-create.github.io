Five Flags

Five Flags is an identity and civic game where players build a personal flag, test it through real-life choices, and learn how self, family, community, country, and future can work together without collapsing into one another.

Return to Products Open Showroom Read Gauges

The game

A civic identity game built around five loyalties.

Five Flags turns identity into a playable structure. Each player builds a five-part flag, then moves through scenario cards that ask what they would protect, what they would sacrifice, where they belong, and how they would act when different loyalties pull in different directions.

Self Personal values, conscience, boundaries, responsibility, and the question: “Who am I when no one is watching?”
Family Origin, care, loyalty, tradition, conflict, inheritance, and the question: “What do I owe the people closest to me?”
Community Friends, school, workplace, neighborhood, teams, service, and the question: “How do I belong without disappearing?”
Country Civic responsibility, law, disagreement, public duty, shared story, and the question: “What does citizenship require from me?”
Future The world players are helping build, the people not yet present, long-range consequences, and the question: “What should survive me?”
Step 1

Build your flag

Players choose symbols, values, colors, and commitments for each of the five flags. The goal is not to make a pretty banner; the goal is to make identity visible.

Step 2

Draw a civic scenario

Scenario cards place players in realistic pressure: a friend needs help, a rule feels unfair, a group disagrees, a family expectation conflicts with a public duty, or the future cost of a choice becomes visible.

Step 3

Choose and explain

Players choose which flag leads the decision, which flag gets protected, and which flag absorbs the cost. The explanation matters as much as the move.

Step 4

Negotiate the table

Other players may challenge, support, trade, question, or offer a better path. The game rewards listening, not just winning an argument.

Step 5

Earn civic strength

Players gain tokens for clarity, courage, fairness, repair, service, wisdom, and accountability. The strongest player is not the loudest; it is the one who can hold identity under pressure.

End state

Build a shared banner

The game ends when players can build a shared civic banner from different individual flags. The win condition is not sameness; it is coordinated difference.

Three versions

Same game. Different depth.

Five Flags keeps the same core structure across every version: five loyalties, pressure cards, explanation, challenge, repair, and shared banner. The difference is the weight of the scenarios, the language of the prompts, and the level of civic complexity.

Kids version

Color, story, and belonging

The kids version uses simple choices, bright flag-building, story prompts, and guided discussion. It teaches children how to name what matters to them without turning every disagreement into a fight.

  • Best for classrooms, families, youth groups, and first civic lessons.
  • Scenarios are concrete: sharing, fairness, helping, apologizing, telling the truth, joining a team.
  • Winning means explaining a choice kindly and helping the table build one shared banner.
Teen version

Identity under pressure

The teen version adds social pressure, disagreement, reputation, online behavior, belonging, leadership, and personal boundaries. It lets players practice adult-level choices before those choices become permanent.

  • Best for schools, clubs, youth leadership, mentorship, and debate settings.
  • Scenarios include peer pressure, family conflict, public speech, group loyalty, exclusion, responsibility, and digital identity.
  • Winning means defending a position, revising it when challenged, and showing how identity can mature without becoming fake.
Adult version

Civic negotiation and consequence

The adult version turns the game into a negotiation surface for families, teams, organizations, and communities. It deals with tradeoffs, leadership, competing duties, institutional trust, and long-range consequence.

  • Best for workshops, leadership training, community rooms, family systems, and public conversation.
  • Scenarios include resource allocation, loyalty conflicts, public trust, repair after harm, competing rights, and future stewardship.
  • Winning means producing a decision that can survive disagreement, not just a decision that feels good in the moment.
What it brings

A playable civic mirror

Five Flags gives people a safe way to see how they make decisions before real conflict forces the issue. It turns identity, responsibility, belonging, and public life into something players can practice.

Gap it closes

Civics without embodiment

Most civic education explains systems from the outside. Five Flags puts the player inside the decision. It asks not only what the rule is, but who the player becomes when the rule costs something.
