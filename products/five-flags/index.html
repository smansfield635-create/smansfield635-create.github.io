Five Flags · Product Chamber

A signal game where the room has to read the truth.

Charades energy. Flag feedback. No explaining.

Five Flags is a family-friendly scene-guessing game where one player, the Flag Master, cannot explain with words. They can only guide the room with signals.

The fun is not just guessing the answer. The fun is watching identity become visible under pressure.

How to play

Simple rules. Strong room energy.

Core rule The Flag Master cannot explain verbally. They cannot define, lecture, hint with full sentences, or give the answer away. They only signal.
Player job The room reads the signals. Guessers adjust their answer based on warmer, colder, hit, start, and reset feedback.
Theme Identity becomes visible under pressure. The room finds out how people think, guess, adapt, react, and recover when language is limited.
Round loop

Draw. Guess. Signal. Adjust. Hit. Reset.

Draw the prompt. The round starts with a scene, identity, role, phrase, situation, or character-type prompt.
The room starts guessing. Guessers speak freely. The Flag Master stays inside the signal boundary.
The Flag Master signals. Warmer means closer. Colder means wrong direction. Hit means the room nailed it.
The room adjusts. The group learns how to read pressure, correction, timing, and each other.
Hit confirms the answer. Once the room gets it, the Flag Master confirms with the hit signal and the next card can begin.
Roles

Everyone has a job.

Flag Master The signal holder. Knows the answer, cannot explain it, and guides the room with limited feedback.
Guessers The room readers. They guess, adapt, test ideas, and learn from the Flag Master’s signals.
Prompts The pressure source. Cards can contain scenes, identities, roles, situations, phrases, or social moments.
Room The shared mirror. The group sees how it thinks under incomplete information.
Why it works

The game is easy to enter and hard to fake.

Family Fast, clean, playable. Simple enough for a living room, flexible enough for different ages.
Party It creates room energy. People laugh because the signal boundary makes the guessing visible.
Classroom It teaches inference. Students practice feedback, communication, category thinking, and adjustment.
Workshop It reveals patterns. Teams can see how people respond to limited feedback, pressure, and correction.
